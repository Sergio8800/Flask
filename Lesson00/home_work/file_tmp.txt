These genes may be passed along to subsequent generations.  All cats, regardless of their appearance, have genes for one or more of the following tabby patterns:</p><!--[P20]--><span class="marker p20"></span><!--[AM20]--><span class="marker AM20 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD20]--><span class="marker MOD20 mod-inline"></span> <ul><li><div><strong>Classic (blotched) tabby</strong>: wide stripes with swirls, or “bull’s-eyes,” on the sides</div></li><li><div><strong>Mackerel tabby</strong>: thinner stripes extending down the sides like the bones of a fish</div></li><li><div><strong>Ticked tabby (agouti)</strong>: <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="alternating" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/alternating" data-type="EB">alternating</a> dark and light bands on each hair strand</div></li><li><div><strong>Spotted</strong>: dark spots on a lighter background with tabby striping on face, legs, and tail</div></li></ul> <p class="topic-paragraph">While many color and pattern combinations are possible, the following are the most important adaptations and factors:</p><!--[P21]--><span class="marker p21"></span><!--[AM21]--><span class="marker AM21 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD21]--><span class="marker MOD21 mod-inline"></span> <ul><li><div><strong>White spotting factor</strong>: This dominant gene gives cats spots or patches of white. Female cats have two X chromosomes and can <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="inherit" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/inherit" data-type="EB">inherit</a> the gene for orange and the gene for black, one on each X chromosome, and therefore can possess the colorful orange and black pattern known as tortoiseshell. But male cats with their one X chromosome can be either orange or black but not both. Only when a male cat has a rare mutation that gives him two XX chromosomes and one Y chromosome can he be tortoiseshell. Such males are usually sterile and often exhibit female behavior.</p><!--[P22]--><span class="marker p22"></span><!--[AM22]--><span class="marker AM22 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD22]--><span class="marker MOD22 mod-inline"></span> </section> </section>  <!--[H4]--><span class="marker h4"></span><section id="ref374456" data-level="1"> <h2 class="h1">Body types and features</h2> <div class="assemblies"><div class="w-100"><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="53055" data-asm-type="image"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper card-media" data-type="image"><a style="min-height: 160px;" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/53/63553-004-C5E6A133/anatomy-cat.jpg" class="position-relative d-flex align-items-center justify-content-center media-overlay-link card-media" data-href="/media/1/168536/53055"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/53/63553-004-C5E6A133/anatomy-cat.jpg" alt="cat" data-width="633" data-height="329" loading="eager"><button class="magnifying-glass btn btn-circle position-absolute shadow btn-white" aria-label="Zoom in"><em class="material-icons link-blue" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><a class="md-assembly-title font-weight-bold mb-5 d-inline-block font-16 font-sans-serif media-overlay-link" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/53/63553-004-C5E6A133/anatomy-cat.jpg" data-href="/media/1/168536/53055">cat</a><div class="md-assembly-caption mt-5 text-muted font-14 font-serif line-clamp"><span>The anatomy of a cat.</span><button class="js-more-btn d-none btn btn-unstyled font-12 bg-white js-content" aria-label="Toggle more/less fact data"><span class="link-blue">(more)</span></button></div></figcaption></figure></div></div><p class="topic-paragraph">Most breeds fall within three general conformation body types: <em>cobby</em>, as seen in the Persian; <em>svelte</em>, as seen in the Siamese; and <em>moderate</em>, as seen in the European Burmese. The cobby type is deep-chested, compact, and broad across the shoulders and rump; the head is large and round. The <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="svelte" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/svelte" data-type="EB">svelte</a> type is slim and lithe, with long tapering lines, a narrow wedge-shaped head, and a long slender tail. Moderate falls between the two extremes.</p><!--[P23]--><span class="marker p23"></span><!--[AM23]--><span class="marker AM23 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD23]--><span class="marker MOD23 mod-inline"></span>  <p class="topic-paragraph">The average length of a full-grown domestic cat is 28 inches (71 cm) for males and 20 inches (51 cm) for females. The average weight of a healthy cat varies from 6 to 12 pounds (2.7 to 5.44 kg). However, size and weight can vary considerably, depending upon the sex and breed. For example, the Savannah cat breed can be up to 17 inches in height (43 cm) and 22 inches in length (56 cm), while members of the <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="Munchkin" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/Munchkin" data-type="EB">Munchkin</a> breed are only 5 to 7 inches (13 to 18 cm) in height because of their short legs. <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Maine-Coon-cat" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Maine Coon cats </a> can weigh up to 22 pounds (10 kg).</p><!--[P24]--><span class="marker p24"></span><!--[AM24]--><span class="marker AM24 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD24]--><span class="marker MOD24 mod-inline"></span>   <section id="ref374457" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Coordination and musculature</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Cats are highly specialized and adaptable carnivorous mammals. They are digitigrade, meaning that they walk on their toes, which is one of the reasons they can walk so silently. Typically, cats walk or run by moving the front and back legs on each side together. Each paw hits the ground at a different point in the stride, which is known as a four-beat gait. Most of the cat’s weight is centered on the front paws while walking.</p><!--[P25]--><span class="marker p25"></span><!--[AM25]--><span class="marker AM25 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD25]--><span class="marker MOD25 mod-inline"></span> <p class="topic-paragraph">Because the vertebrae of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/vertebral-column" class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true">spinal column</a> are held together by muscles rather than by ligaments, the cat can move with great agility. The construction of the shoulder joints permits the cat to turn its foreleg in almost any direction, allowing for quick turns when chasing prey. Cats are so well coordinated that they almost invariably land on their feet if they fall or are dropped.</p><!--[P26]--><span class="marker p26"></span><!--[AM26]--><span class="marker AM26 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD26]--><span class="marker MOD26 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374458" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Teeth</h2> <div class="assemblies"><div class="w-100"><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="298980" data-asm-type="image"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper card-media" data-type="image"><a style="min-height: 160px;" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/70/234870-050-D4D024BB/Orange-colored-cat-yawns-displaying-teeth.jpg" class="position-relative d-flex align-items-center justify-content-center media-overlay-link card-media" data-href="/media/1/168536/298980"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/70/234870-050-D4D024BB/Orange-colored-cat-yawns-displaying-teeth.jpg?w=300" alt="cat teeth" data-width="1600" data-height="1146" loading="eager"><button class="magnifying-glass btn btn-circle position-absolute shadow btn-white" aria-label="Zoom in"><em class="material-icons link-blue" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><a class="md-assembly-title font-weight-bold mb-5 d-inline-block font-16 font-sans-serif media-overlay-link" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/70/234870-050-D4D024BB/Orange-colored-cat-yawns-displaying-teeth.jpg" data-href="/media/1/168536/298980">cat teeth</a><div class="md-assembly-caption mt-5 text-muted font-14 font-serif line-clamp"><span>Cat yawning and displaying teeth. </span><button class="js-more-btn d-none btn btn-unstyled font-12 bg-white js-content" aria-label="Toggle more/less fact data"><span class="link-blue">(more)</span></button></div></figcaption></figure></div></div><p class="topic-paragraph">A cat’s teeth are adapted to three functions: stabbing, anchoring, and cutting. Cats have no flat-crowned crushing teeth and therefore cannot chew their food; instead, they cut it up. Except for the canines and molars, the cat’s teeth are more or less nonfunctional; most of the cheek teeth do not even meet when the mouth is closed.</p><!--[P27]--><span class="marker p27"></span><!--[AM27]--><span class="marker AM27 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD27]--><span class="marker MOD27 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374459" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Claws</h2> <div class="assemblies"><div class="w-100"><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="145171" data-asm-type="image"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper card-media" data-type="image"><a style="min-height: 160px;" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/56/93056-050-2034DA41/Diagram-cat-claw.jpg" class="position-relative d-flex align-items-center justify-content-center media-overlay-link card-media" data-href="/media/1/168536/145171"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/56/93056-050-2034DA41/Diagram-cat-claw.jpg" alt="cat claw" data-width="1600" data-height="695" loading="eager"><button class="magnifying-glass btn btn-circle position-absolute shadow btn-white" aria-label="Zoom in"><em class="material-icons link-blue" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><a class="md-assembly-title font-weight-bold mb-5 d-inline-block font-16 font-sans-serif media-overlay-link" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/56/93056-050-2034DA41/Diagram-cat-claw.jpg" data-href="/media/1/168536/145171">cat claw</a><div class="md-assembly-caption mt-5 text-muted font-14 font-serif line-clamp"><span>Diagram of a cat's claw.</span><button class="js-more-btn d-none btn btn-unstyled font-12 bg-white js-content" aria-label="Toggle more/less fact data"><span class="link-blue">(more)</span></button></div></figcaption></figure></div></div><p class="topic-paragraph">The cat’s claws retract when not in use. The action that unsheathes the claws also spreads the toes widely, making the foot more than twice as broad as it normally is and converting it into a <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="formidable" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/formidable" data-type="MW">formidable</a> weapon. This claw-sheathing mechanism is present in all species of the cat family except the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/cheetah-mammal" class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true">cheetah</a>. Although there are no nerve endings in the nail itself, blood capillaries are present in the inner, “quick” area. This is why, when trimming the nails of domestic cats, pet owners should cut the sharp white tip of the nail but not the pink quick, since cutting the latter can be painful for a cat.</p><!--[P28]--><span class="marker p28"></span><!--[AM28]--><span class="marker AM28 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD28]--><span class="marker MOD28 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374460" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Ears</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Not only is the cat’s hearing acute, but its outer ears are equipped with more than a dozen muscles that allow the ears to swivel 180 degrees. This allows the ears to pivot toward the source of sounds, and the ear shape helps funnel sounds into the ears. This design also makes it easier to precisely locate the source of sounds.</p><!--[P29]--><span class="marker p29"></span><!--[AM29]--><span class="marker AM29 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD29]--><span class="marker MOD29 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374461" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Tail</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">The tail is part of the spine and generally contains an additional 20-some caudal vertebrae. The exception is breeds such as the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Manx-cat" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Manx</a> and the Japanese Bobtail, which have mutations that give them short tails or no tails at all. The cat’s tail is mobile and used for both balance and communication. It acts as a counterweight, but it is the cat’s sense of <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="equilibrium" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/equilibrium" data-type="MW">equilibrium</a> and its flexible spine that allow it to self-right when falling.</p><!--[P30]--><span class="marker p30"></span><!--[AM30]--><span class="marker AM30 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD30]--><span class="marker MOD30 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374462" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Skin and hair</h2> <div class="assemblies"><div class="link-blue">(more)</span></button></div></figcaption></figure></div></div><p class="topic-paragraph">The cat’s skin is composed of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/dermis" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">dermis</a> and the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/epidermis-anatomy" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">epidermis</a>. Tiny erector muscles, attached to <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/hair-anatomy" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">hair follicles</a>, enable the cat to bristle. Even though the cat is a relatively small animal, it can frighten enemies by arching its back and bristling. In addition, bristling creates air pockets that protect the cat against cold. Regardless of coat length, all cats (except for such breeds as the Cornish Rex and the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Rex-cat" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Devon Rex</a> and hairless cats, such as the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Sphynx-cat" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Sphynx</a>) have three hair types: guard, awn, and down. The guard hairs are long and stiff and cover the top layer of the cat’s coat, keeping it dry. Down and awn hairs are secondary hairs (also called the undercoat) and are more numerous than guard hairs. Awn hairs are thin and usually have stiff pointed ends. Down hairs are the most numerous; they are very fine and mat the easiest.</p><!--[P31]--><span class="marker p31"></span><!--[AM31]--><span class="marker AM31 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD31]--><span class="marker MOD31 mod-inline"></span> </section> </section>  <!--[H5]--><span class="marker h5"></span><section id="ref374463" data-level="1"> <h2 class="h1">Senses</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Cats, like humans, have five <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/senses" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">senses</a>. The whiskers (vibrissae) are touch receptors that are <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="vital" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vital" data-type="EB">vital</a> to the cat’s hunting ability. The eyebrows and hairs of the cheek, chin, legs, and ears are extremely sensitive to vibrations and provide cats with a great deal of information. This cat “radar” is vital to the cat’s ability to move about and hunt in low-light conditions. The paw pads are also acutely sensitive and help the cat when grasping prey.</p><!--[P33]--><span class="marker p33"></span><!--[AM33]--><span class="marker AM33 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD33]--><span class="marker MOD33 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374465" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Hearing and “seeing Martians”</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Cats have a keen sense of hearing. Their ears are mobile and can move independently of one other, allowing cats to pinpoint sounds with accuracy. The cat’s hearing is tuned to higher frequencies because the sounds of its usual prey are generally higher pitched. They can detect frequencies both above and below the range humans can detect.</p><!--[P34]--><span class="marker p34"></span><!--[AM34]--><span class="marker AM34 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD34]--><span class="marker MOD34 mod-inline"></span> <p class="topic-paragraph">The cat’s superior hearing may explain the phenomenon sometimes called “seeing Martians.” For example, when a domestic cat suddenly class="md-assembly-caption mt-5 text-muted font-14 font-serif line-clamp"><span>“Glowing” cat eyes showing the tapetum lucidum, a reflective layer behind the retina that increases the amount of light available for night vision in many nocturnal vertebrates.</span><button class="js-more-btn d-none btn btn-unstyled font-12 bg-white js-content" aria-label="Toggle more/less fact data"><span class="link-blue">(more)</span></button></div></figcaption></figure></div></div><p class="topic-paragraph">Domestic cats are “crepuscular”—active at dawn and dusk. The pupils of the eyes expand or contract according to the intensity of light. Cats have nictitating membranes (third eyelids) that protect the eyeballs from damage. Color perception is not highly developed in cats, likely because it is unimportant to their survival; detecting a prey animal’s movements is more important than observing its colors.</p><!--[P39]--><span class="marker p39"></span><!--[AM39]--><span class="marker AM39 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD39]--><span class="marker MOD39 mod-inline"></span> <p class="topic-paragraph">Despite popular belief, the eyes of domestic cats do not actually glow in the dark. When a cat’s eyes shine, light is bouncing off a layer of cells behind the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/retina" class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true">retina</a> called the tapetum lucidum. This mechanism allows cats to use ambient light very efficiently. Because domestic cats are crepuscular hunters, they need only one-sixth of the illumination levels humans require to see well.</p><!--[P40]--><span class="marker p40"></span><!--[AM40]--><span class="marker AM40 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD40]--><span class="marker MOD40 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374469" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Cats’ “sixth sense”</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Although experts doubt the existence of this “sixth sense,” many <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="anecdotal" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/anecdotal" data-type="MW">anecdotal</a> accounts contend that cats have an unidentified sense that allows them to find their way home, sometimes over distances of hundreds of miles. No one knows how cats accomplish this feat, but theories abound, including that cats use Earth’s magnetic fields to negotiate their way back to familiar <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/territory-ecology" class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true">territory</a>.</p><!--[P41]--><span class="marker p41"></span><!--[AM41]--><span class="marker AM41 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD41]--><span class="marker MOD41 mod-inline"></span> </section> </section>  <!--[H6]--><span class="marker h6"></span><section id="ref374470" data-level="1"> <h2 class="h1">Behavior</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Cats are often considered aloof or unaffectionate, but that is a misunderstanding of their nature. They are solitary hunters but are nevertheless social animals. As long as they are not competing for limited food resources, they can form close-knit groups that are usually composed of the mother cat and her kittens and related females. Unneutered males roam, sometimes widely, seeking out mating opportunities.</p><!--[P42]--><span class="marker p42"></span><!--[AM42]--><span class="marker AM42 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD42]--><span class="marker MOD42 mod-inline"></span>  <section id="ref374471" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Elimination habits</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Burying urine and feces in loose soil is normal behavior for cats. Predators and dominant cats locate by scent, which is why domestic cats may <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="avoid" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/avoid" data-type="EB">avoid</a> dirty litter boxes. Also, dominant cats spray urine and leave their wastes uncovered to mark their territory.</p><!--[P43]--><span class="marker p43"></span><!--[AM43]--><span class="marker AM43 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD43]--><span class="marker MOD43 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374472" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Sexual behavior</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Cats reach reproductive age between 7 and 12 months. If not altered, they spend a good deal of their time seeking out sexual partners, fighting off rivals, and raising the subsequent offspring. “Tomcats” (sexually mature males prone to fighting over mates) are not particular about the age, breed, or kinship of mating partners; they readily mate with related females.</p><!--[P44]--><span class="marker p44"></span><!--[AM44]--><span class="marker AM44 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD44]--><span class="marker MOD44 mod-inline"></span> <p class="topic-paragraph">A breeding female (queen) can come into heat as many as five times a year. Domestic cats are induced ovulators, which means that <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/ovulation" class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true">ovulation</a> does not occur until the queen mates. This efficient system vastly increases the chances that a cat will <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="conceive" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conceive" data-type="EB">conceive</a>, which is one of the reasons for cat overpopulation.</p><!--[P45]--><span class="marker p45"></span><!--[AM45]--><span class="marker AM45 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD45]--><span class="marker MOD45 mod-inline"></span> <p class="topic-paragraph">The <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/gestation" class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true">gestation</a> period is about 65 days. Kittens are born blind, deaf, and helpless; their senses begin to function about 10 days after birth. Kittens at birth may lack distinctive coloring. For example, Siamese kittens are white at birth and begin to develop the characteristic “points” (distinct dark areas) at about three weeks.</p><!--[P46]--><span class="marker p46"></span><!--[AM46]--><span class="marker AM46 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD46]--><span class="marker MOD46 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374473" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Body language and communication</h2> <div class="assemblies"><div class="w-100"><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="233230" data-asm-type="image"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><a class="md-assembly-title font-weight-bold mb-5 d-inline-block font-16 font-sans-serif media-overlay-link" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/25/172925-050-DC7E2298/black-cat-back.jpg" data-href="/media/1/168536/233230">black cat</a><div class="md-assembly-caption mt-5 text-muted font-14 font-serif line-clamp"><span>Black cat arching its back and bristling its fur.</span><button class="js-more-btn d-none btn btn-unstyled font-12 bg-white js-content" aria-label="Toggle more/less fact data"><span class="link-blue">(more)</span></button></div></figcaption></figure></div></div><p class="topic-paragraph">A cat communicates in a variety of bodily ways:</p><!--[P47]--><span class="marker p47"></span><!--[AM47]--><span class="marker AM47 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD47]--><span class="marker MOD47 mod-inline"></span> <ul><li><div><strong>Arching</strong>: A frightened or angry cat arches its back, turns its body sideways, and bends its tail into an upside-down U to give the impression of ferocity and greater size. It twitches its ears down and to the side to detect attacks from the side and rear.</div></li><li><div><strong>Bristling (pilomotor reflex)</strong>: Bristling the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/fur-animal-skin" class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true">fur</a> makes a cat look bigger and more menacing to other cats or predators.</div></li><li><div><strong>Crouching</strong>: A frightened, submissive cat cringes low to protect its <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="vulnerable" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vulnerable" data-type="MW">vulnerable</a> underbelly and to appear smaller and less threatening. Its ears and whiskers flatten, and it holds its tail close to the ground. A cat also crouches when about to pounce on prey or a toy, but the ears are usually cocked forward, the whiskers are spread wide, and the tail tip is flicking with excitement.</div></li><li><div><strong>Kneading</strong>: Also called “making biscuits,” kneading is a form of scent marking via the scent glands in the paw pads. Kittens knead to help stimulate milk production, and adult cats knead as a form of self-soothing or as a sign of affection.</div></li><li><div><strong>Tail raising</strong>: Holding the tail erect generally indicates a friendly greeting. It means that the cat is feeling calm and confident.</div></li><li><div><strong>Swishing/Lashing</strong>: Swishing the tail from side to side means that a cat is angry or is about to attack in a predatory manner. </div></li><li><div><strong>Tucking</strong>: When the tail is tucked between the hind legs, a cat is signaling fear, <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="submission" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/submission" data-type="EB">submission</a>, or defeat.</div></li><li><div><strong>Twitching</strong>: Mild irritation is generally indicated when the tail is held still but the tip is twitching.</div></li><li><div><strong>Swiveling</strong>: Sexual invitation by a female in heat is indicated when the tail is held to one side.</div></li><li><div><strong>Spraying</strong>: Both male and female cats can spray: they back up to vertical surfaces, raise their tails, and spray urine. This is marking behavior but can also be a sign that a cat feels threatened.</div></li><li><div><strong>Scent marking</strong>: Cats have scent glands on the temples, the gape of the mouth, the chin, the root of the tail, and in the anal area. The scents give cats information about one another, and secretions from the glands are used to mark cats’ perceived territory and belongings via rubbing. Scent glands on the underside of the paws leave their personal scent on scratched items.</div></li></ul> </section>  <section id="ref374474" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Vocal communication</h2> data-icon="keyboard_arrow_right"></span></button></div><p class="topic-paragraph">Cats use a variety of sounds to communicate with other cats and with their human companions. These sounds can be interpreted as greetings, demands, pleas, challenges, or warnings, depending on the tone.</p><!--[P48]--><span class="marker p48"></span><!--[AM48]--><span class="marker AM48 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD48]--><span class="marker MOD48 mod-inline"></span> <ul><li><div><strong>Meowing</strong>: This is usually interpreted as a greeting, plea, or complaint; the cat may be hungry, lonely, or <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="bored" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/bored" data-type="EB">bored</a>. However, some cat breeds vocalize more than others and use meowing and other sounds to engage with their owners.</div></li><li><div><strong>Purring</strong>: This is one of the most recognized sounds cats make and is usually interpreted as a sound of contentment or affection. However, cats also “distress purr” when hungry, lonely, in pain, ill, or even dying.</div></li><li><div><strong>Hissing</strong>: When threatened, cats expel air sharply through their mouths in a distinctive sound called hissing. Hissing is usually intended as a warning and means that the cat is frightened or angry.</div></li><li><div><strong>Spitting</strong>: Spitting is similar to hissing but has a sharper and more explosive sound.</div></li><li><div><strong>Growling</strong>: This sound ranges from low rumbles in the throat to open-mouthed growls. All cats, wild and domestic, growl as a warning. Although cats will occasionally growl playfully, body language will indicate whether they are sincerely frightened or angry.</div></li><li><div><strong>Screaming</strong>: A screaming cat is thoroughly angry or very frightened. Never try to handle a screaming cat; a cat that is angry or frightened may not be able to recognize friend from foe and may injure.</div></li><li><div><strong>Yowling</strong>: This sound is usually interpreted as a plea or demand. The cat may be hungry, bored, lonely, in pain, or (if an unaltered female) in heat. Yowling can be annoying, particularly if the cat does it often, and it may indicate a health concern. Continuous yowling can be a sign of <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="cognitive" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cognitive" data-type="MW">cognitive</a> decline in senior cats.</div></li><li><div><strong>Chattering</strong>: Chattering is a staccato sound generally made when a cat spots prey that is out of reach. This sound is usually interpreted as a sign of frustration or desire. Chattering can also be soundless.</div></li><li><div><strong>Mating calls</strong>: Generally called “caterwauling,” these loud long yowls are made by females in heat and are intended to attract prospective mates.</div></li></ul> </section> </section>  <!--[H7]--><span class="marker h7"></span><section id="ref374475" data-level="1"> <h2 class="h1">Heredity</h2>  <section id="ref374476" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Genes and chromosomes</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph"><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/gene" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Genes</a> are responsible for the determination and transmission of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/heredity-genetics" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">hereditary</a> characteristics. Each gene inhabits a particular site (locus) on a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/chromosome" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">chromosome</a>, and, because cats have two of each chromosome, they also have two copies of each gene. These genes determine the genetic makeup of the cat, called the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/genotype" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">genotype</a>. Domestic cats have 19 pairs of chromosomes and, like humans, one pair of sex chromosomes determining their sex (two X chromosomes for females, one X and one Y chromosome for males).</p><!--[P49]--><span class="marker p49"></span><!--[AM49]--><span class="marker AM49 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD49]--><span class="marker MOD49 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374477" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Dominant and recessive genes</h2> <p class="topic-paragraph">Cats, like all other sexually reproducing animals, inherit half of their genetic makeup from each parent. The paired gene copies may be different from each other and cause variations in characteristics. When both copies of a particular gene are the same, the associated trait will be present (expressed) in the cat’s physical appearance. If the cat has two different gene copies for a particular trait, the dominant gene will have a masking or overriding effect on the recessive gene. A recessive gene will be expressed in the physical appearance if two copies of it are inherited. For example, long hair in cats is a recessive trait; a cat must inherit the gene for long hair from both parents to have long hair.</p><!--[P50]--><span class="marker p50"></span><!--[AM50]--><span class="marker AM50 am-inline"></span><div class="one-good-fact-module">
</div><!--[MOD50]--><span class="marker MOD50 mod-inline"></span> </section>  <section id="ref374478" data-level="2"> <h2 class="h2">Mutation and new breeds</h2> <div class="assemblies"><div class="w-100"><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="274097" data-asm-type="image"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper card-media" data-type="image"><a style="min-height: 160px;" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/36/234736-050-4AC5B6D5/Scottish-fold-cat.jpg" class="position-relative d-flex align-items-center justify-content-center media-overlay-link card-media" data-href="/media/1/168536/274097"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/36/234736-050-4AC5B6D5/Scottish-fold-cat.jpg?w=300" alt="Scottish Fold cat" data-width="1600" data-height="1067" loading="eager"><button class="magnifying-glass btn btn-circle position-absolute shadow btn-white" aria-label="Zoom in"><em class="material-icons link-blue" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><a class="md-assembly-title font-weight-bold mb-5 d-inline-block font-16 font-sans-serif media-overlay-link" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/36/234736-050-4AC5B6D5/Scottish-fold-cat.jpg" data-href="/media/1/168536/274097">Scottish Fold cat</a><div class="md-assembly-caption mt-5 text-muted font-14 font-serif line-clamp"><span>Scottish Fold cat exhibiting a gene mutation causing the ears to fold.</span><button class="js-more-btn d-none btn btn-unstyled font-12 bg-white js-content" aria-label="Toggle more/less fact data"><span class="link-blue">(more)</span></button></div></figcaption></figure></div></div><p class="topic-paragraph">A gene at a particular <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="locus" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/locus" data-type="EB">locus</a> on a chromosome may have more than one form, because genes can mutate, and these changes may be passed onto future generations. The existence of these alternate forms allows for variety in cat conformation, color, and coat. Some mutations have positive or neutral effects, but not all do. For example, the mutation that causes the ears to fold in the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Scottish-fold-cat" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Scottish Fold breed</a> can also cause a bone and cartilage abnormality called osteochondrodysplasia.</p><!--[P51]--><span class="marker p51"></span><!--[AM51]--><span class="marker AM51 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD51]--><span class="marker MOD51 mod-inline"></span> </section> </section> <!--[END-OF-CONTENT]--><span class="marker end-of-content"></span><!--[AFTER-ARTICLE]--><span class="marker after-article"></span></div>
